SOOWAHLIE FIRST NATION Th'éwá:lí

Intro to About Page

Soowalie First Nation is approximately 1,100 acres in size, the community is situated in the Fraser Valley Regional District, located between the towns of Vedder Crossing and Cultus Lake, approximately 1.5 hours East of Vancouver, British Columbia, Canada.

The Soowahlie First Nation has 373 members, 155 of those members are of the voting age. approximately 157 members live on reserve with the other 216 living off reserve. There are 69 houses on Soowahlie. Soowahlie is located near Cultus Lake, which is about a ten-minute drive from the city of Chilliwack. Soowahlie's original traditional land included Cultus Lake and used to go to the border of the city of Chilliwack.

Soowahlie First Nation has a Band Administration office, a Health Office and a number of Economic Development initiatives. The administration basic services are provided to the on-reserve membership. INAC limits the services to the off-reserve membership. The Administration and Council engages in the development of annual budgets, the Council 5-Year Plan, planning for all programs, community consultation, capacity building of the Council and staff, developing codes and policies, ect.

Soowahlie Vision and history

“The Stó:lō are the original inhabitants of the Fraser Valley; they have been here since time immemorial and continue to live here today. The villages along the Fraser River housed hundreds of people in large structures called longhouses that have stood for hundreds of years in one location-passed down from one generation to the next. Prior to initial contact with European newcomers, the Stó:lō population was estimated to number up to 30,000 people.”

SFU Museum of Archeology, A Journey Into Time Immemorial.

Soowahlie (Th'éwá:lí) has connections to Nooksack in present-day Washington State. Soowahlie's (Th'éwá:lí's) origin stories suggest that the community is the result of an amalgamation of Nooksack and Chilliwack (Ts'elxweyéqw) peoples.

Swí:lhcha (Cultus Lake) is a freshwater lake abundant in resources and rich in stories. It is central to the history and identity of Soowahlie (Th'éwá:lí). Today it is also the centerpiece of two parks: Cultus Lake Park and Cultus Lake Provincial Park.

“According to oral histories, Soowahlie (Th'éwá:lí) is the product of migration, and a hybrid of two separate communities brought together by a natural disaster. The late Elder Amy Cooper explained that a long time ago there was a great famine in the area. Because of the drought, the roots, wild potatoes, berries and fish, staple life sources for Xwélmexw peoples, died off and did not grow that year. Everyone in the area near what is now Cultus Lake (Swí:lhcha) died from starvation except for one woman. Eventually, a man from Whatcom Lake in Nooksack territory (in present-day Washington State) travelled north and met her where she lived. They married, and their union was the start of the community of Soowahlie (Th'éwá:lí). This story is closely related to the origin story of Swí:lhcha.”

SFU Museum of Archeology, A Journey Into Time Immemorial. http://web.uvic.ca/~strimble/stolo/

As history shows that Soowahlie frequented the entire Chilliwack River Watershed. Depending on the season, Villages could be found anywhere along the Chilliwack River. Some of the archive's information reveals that as far back as 10,000 years ago, the Soowahlie people have lived in this area. As stated earlier, we have always been in this area taking care of the natural resources and we intend to continue with this long-standing connection to the land. We feel that we have all of the abilities to proceed and fulfill our goals as caretakers of the land. It is part of Chief and Council's mandate to be involved in all aspects of the land, water, fishing and forest industry.
